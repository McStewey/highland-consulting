FIELD NOTE 01AI + TECHNOLOGY

Don’t start with the tool.
Start with the work.

A useful AI conversation does not begin with, “Where can we use AI?” It begins with, “Where is valuable work slower, harder, less consistent, or less informed than it should be?”

Starting with the tool invites scattered experiments. Starting with the work reveals a system: who makes the decision, what information they need, where time is lost, what good judgment looks like, and what must remain human. That is where a strong use case takes shape.

Automation without operating clarity makes confusion move faster.

The practical sequence is simple: make the workflow visible, define the outcome, identify the point of leverage, design the human and technical roles together, test with real users, and measure the change. AI then becomes part of an operating improvement—not a side project looking for relevance.

FIELD NOTE 02OPERATIONS

Your org chart is not
your operating model.

An org chart can tell you who reports to whom. It cannot tell you how a customer request moves, who can make a trade-off, what happens when two priorities collide, or whether the right information reaches the right person in time.

An operating model connects strategy to daily work. It clarifies the capabilities the business needs, where accountability sits, how decisions get made, how processes cross functions, which technology enables them, and how performance is seen and managed.

When growth exposes friction, leaders often redraw boxes. Sometimes that helps. More often, the real repair is in the white space: decision rights, meeting rhythm, data, handoffs, measures, and the rules people use when the written process meets reality.

FIELD NOTE 03GROWTH + CAPITAL

Capital-ready is an
operating condition.

A compelling vision may open a door. Readiness is what keeps the conversation moving. Funders, partners, and grantmakers need to see how the opportunity becomes a controlled, accountable execution plan.

That evidence lives across the business: a coherent legal and partnership structure, realistic sources and uses, credible assumptions, clear milestones, known dependencies, accountable owners, a defensible risk picture, and reporting that can survive scrutiny.

The work of becoming capital-ready often improves the project itself. Ambiguity is surfaced earlier. Roles become clearer. Gaps are resolved before they become expensive. The financing strategy and the operating strategy become one story.

FIELD NOTE 04PEOPLE

Change resistance is often
design feedback.

People do resist change. But “resistance” is too blunt an explanation for what is often a rational response to unclear direction, added workload, conflicting incentives, weak tools, or a solution designed without the people expected to run it.

Treat friction as information. Ask what people are protecting, what they cannot see, what the new process makes harder, and where the design contradicts the stated outcome. Then involve the people closest to the work in making the solution usable.

Adoption is not a communication plan added at the end. It is a design requirement from the beginning.
